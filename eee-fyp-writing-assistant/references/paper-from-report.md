# KF-04: Transforming a Technical Report into a Technical Paper

This guide walks through the process of condensing an FYP technical report (~50–100
pages) into a technical paper (9–12 pages). The transformation is not simply about
cutting text — it requires re-thinking what matters most about the project and
restructuring the presentation around contributions and results.

## Mindset Shift

| Report Mindset | Paper Mindset |
|---|---|
| Document everything that was done | Foreground what was contributed |
| Show you understand the field (broad lit review) | Position your work in the field (focused gap analysis) |
| Describe procedures step by step | Describe approach at a level needed to evaluate it |
| Reflect on difficulties and learning | Report limitations as they affect results |
| Serve your supervisor (who knows the project) | Serve a broader audience (who does not) |

---

## Step-by-Step Transformation Process

### Step 1: Identify the Core Contribution

Before touching the report, answer these questions:
- What is the single most important result or deliverable?
- What is novel — what has not been done before, or what has been done better?
- If a reader remembers only one thing from this paper, what should it be?

The answers to these questions define the paper's centre of gravity. Everything in the
paper should point toward or support these contributions.

### Step 2: Decide What to Keep, Condense, and Cut

| Report Section | Paper Action |
|---|---|
| **Abstract** | Rewrite from scratch for the paper (write it last) |
| **Introduction — broad background** | Cut heavily. Keep only what is needed to motivate the problem (1–2 paragraphs) |
| **Introduction — objectives** | Keep and sharpen. State contributions explicitly |
| **Literature review** | Condense to ~1 page. Keep only studies directly related to the research gap. For each, state what it did and what it missed |
| **Theoretical principles** | Integrate key theory into Methodology or Introduction. Cut derivations available in standard references; cite them instead |
| **Analysis of Problem** | Merge relevant parts into Introduction (problem statement) or Methodology |
| **Method of Investigation** | Condense. Keep what the reader needs to understand and evaluate the approach. Cut routine procedures |
| **Design / Construction** | Condense. Focus on novel design decisions and their justifications. Use block diagrams instead of detailed schematics |
| **Results** | Reorganise by topic, not chronology. Lead with the most important findings. Foreground hypothesis testing, targets achieved, deliverables validated |
| **Discussion** | Merge with Results (unless separation aids clarity). Cut cost-effectiveness if not central. Keep: comparison with theory, critical evaluation, limitations |
| **Conclusion** | Rewrite for the paper. Add significance statement. Cut reflective content |
| **Appendices** | Eliminate entirely — papers do not have appendices. Any essential data should be in the body; the rest is cut |

### Step 3: Restructure into Paper Sections

Map the condensed content into the paper structure defined in KF-02:

1. Title
2. Author & Affiliation
3. Abstract + Index Terms
4. Introduction (merged: background + objectives + contributions + paper overview)
5. Related Work (condensed literature review)
6. Methodology / Design (merged and condensed)
7. Results and Discussion (merged or separate; organised by topic)
8. Conclusion
9. Acknowledgement
10. References

### Step 4: Rewrite, Don't Just Cut

Simply deleting paragraphs from the report produces choppy, incoherent text.
Each section of the paper should be **rewritten** with paper-appropriate density:

- Shorter paragraphs (no paragraph-level mini-introductions or mini-conclusions)
- More compact sentences
- Information-dense figures and tables that replace prose
- Specific, informative subheadings that help the reader scan

### Step 5: Redesign Visuals

Report figures are often full-page or half-page. Paper figures must be readable at
column width (~3.4 in) or at most two-column width (~7 in). Consider:

- Can two related report figures be combined into one composite figure?
- Are axis labels, legends, and annotations still readable at the smaller size?
- Can a table replace a verbose paragraph of numerical results?
- Can a block diagram replace a detailed schematic (with the schematic available
  upon request but not in the paper)?

### Step 6: Verify Cohesion

After restructuring, read the paper from start to finish and check:
- Does the Introduction lead naturally into the Related Work?
- Does the Methodology explain everything needed to understand the Results?
- Does the Results section deliver on the promises made in the Introduction?
- Does the Conclusion synthesise (not repeat) the Results?
- Are all figures and tables called out before they appear?
- Are all abbreviations defined at first use?
- Are all claims cited?

### Step 7: Check the Page Budget

Estimate the page count for each section. If the paper exceeds 12 pages:
- Cut background material in the Introduction
- Reduce the number of figures (keep only the most informative)
- Condense the Methodology further
- Tighten prose throughout

If the paper is significantly under 9 pages, the Results and Discussion section
likely needs more depth — more interpretation, more comparison with theory, or
additional supporting data.

## Common Pitfalls in Report-to-Paper Transformation

1. **Keeping report-length introductions.** A paper introduction is 1–1.5 pages, not 5–10.
2. **Preserving chronological Results.** Organise by importance and topic, not by when
   the experiment was run.
3. **Forgetting to state contributions.** The paper must explicitly say what is new.
   A report often implies this; a paper must state it.
4. **Leaving in reflective content.** "I learned that..." and "In hindsight..." belong
   in a report, not a paper. Frame difficulties as limitations on the results.
5. **Keeping all figures from the report.** Select the 5–8 most informative figures.
   Combine, redesign, or cut the rest.
6. **Not rewriting the abstract.** The report abstract and paper abstract serve different
   purposes. Always rewrite from scratch after the paper is complete.
7. **Including appendices.** Papers do not have appendices. If the data cannot fit in
   the body at paper density, it is not essential to the paper.
